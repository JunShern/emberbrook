# EMBERBROOK — Story Bible

Canonical lore for the ten-chapter arc. Chapter One ("Emberwake", `public/js/chapter1.js`) is
fixed; everything below exists to explain it, not to revise it. When writing dialogue, prefer
the plain-knowledge register in §7 — villagers talk about lamps the way sailors talk about tide.

**Premise in one breath:** In the valley villages, people keep their own memories like anyone
else — what they give to the communal flame is the *heart* of them: joys and griefs told once
and given over, which the flame radiates back through the street lamps as one shared warmth.
One family per village is paid the opposite duty: to keep the flame, and to carry their own
hearts the hard way. When Emberbrook's flame is carried off in a single heartbeat — leaving a
village that knows every fact of itself and feels none of them — the only two people whose
names still feel like their own — a lamplighter who never swore his vow, and a mapmaker who
dreams roads she's never walked — must carry the last live spark back to the fire it came from.

---

## 1. The Kindling and the Order of Lamplighters (deep background)

Deep in the Whisperwood burns the **Kindling**: the first fire, older than any village, burning
in a stone bowl in a clearing. It is a fire that *keeps* — not the things told to it, but what
they weighed. Tell it a grief and the grief is truly given over; tell it a joy and the joy is
banked. The fire holds the felt residue of every telling, forever, and warms everyone in reach
of its light with all of it. People have always brought it their dead, their weddings, their
bad winters.

The **Order of Lamplighters** grew up around it: walkers who carried seed-embers of the Kindling
out of the wood and lit a **Heartlight** in each valley — a daughter-fire with the same keeping
nature, seated in crystal on a stone pedestal so it could burn without fuel. The Order's whole
creed is one sentence: ***light does not die — it is only ever carried.*** Fire from the Kindling
is never "made" and never "put out"; it is passed, hand to hand, wick to wick.

The Order always walked in **pairs**: a **Flamebearer**, who carried the seed-ember, and a
**Waykeeper**, who carried the roads in their head — because the Whisperwood does not hold still,
and no drawn map of it survives a season. *"The map does not know fire. The flame does not know
the way."* (Rowan quotes this in Chapter One without knowing he's quoting.) Every gate the Order
built — including Emberbrook's Old Gate — is cut with **twin sigils** that open only for two
people standing them together, because that road was never meant to be walked alone.

Three hundred years ago the Kindling began to fail — burning low, blue, cold. The Order's
walkers were recalled to tend it. Most refused: they had villages now, neighbors, children. They
sealed the gates from the valley side (standard protocol against the wood's moths), stayed on as
resident lamplighter families, and the annual circuit — the re-lighting walk from the Kindling to
every Heartlight — simply stopped. One officer stayed with the mother-fire: the **Warden of the
Kindling**. He is still there. He is the Stranger with the pale-blue lantern.

## 2. The Lamplighter Tradition — what Lake's job actually is

### The Heartlight
Emberbrook's Heartlight is a seed of the Kindling, carried into the valley three hundred years
ago and lit on the pedestal in the square the day the village was founded. Like its mother, it
keeps what it is told — not the moments, the *meaning* of them. Three hundred years of feeling
live in it: the joy of every wedding, the trust remade after every argument, every good loaf and
bad winter given over and given back. It is not an archive; nothing can be looked up in it. It
is the village's heart, held in common — and it **radiates**. Stand in its light and you stand
inside three centuries of your neighbors' best and worst days, resolved into one warmth. The
power of this is enormous in the quietest way: camaraderie, shared identity, wisdom that stays
learned (Emberbrook does not repeat its old mistakes, because the understanding that ended them
is alive in the light), motivation, low strife. An individual's gift becomes the community's
anchor.

By design, there is nothing to snoop: a telling dissolves into the radiance anonymously, like a
cup of water into a pond. The flame gives back warmth, never moments. Nobody — not the teller,
not a neighbor, not the flame itself — can fish a particular evening back out.

### The lamps
The street lamps are not decoration and not (only) illumination. The Heartlight is the
reservoir; the lamps are how the warmth reaches you. Each lamp is lit *from* the Heartlight's
line of fire, and while a lamp burns it does two concrete things:

1. **It carries the radiance.** The shared warmth travels the flame, and the flame lives in the
   lamps: a lit lamp bathes its street in everything the village has ever given over. Streets
   with dead lamps go *dull* — out of the light, the communal warmth fades for you: neighbors
   feel a little more like strangers, mornings start slow and pointless, old quarrels itch.
   Everyone keeps every memory they ever had — they just stop being warmed by anyone else's.
   Villagers know this the way they know damp gives you a cough. "Dark lamp, dull street" is
   not a proverb; it is a mechanism.
2. **It wards off moths.** Moths (see §3) nestle into unwarded light and sleeping minds and fray
   what they touch. A properly lit lamp is a closed door to them.

### The rounds
Every dusk, the lamplighter takes the brass lighter down from above the hearth and walks the
circuit in fixed order — low ground first (the pond lane, where moths rise off the water at
dusk), then inward, ending at the lamps nearest the Heartlight: *closing the ring* before full
dark. This is the job, stated plainly: **carry the flame from the village's heart to a lamppost
near every home, so that nobody sleeps outside the warmth.** At each lamp he opens the little
brass door, touches flame to wick, **and means it**.

"Meaning it" is not poetry — under this model it is obvious: a lamp's whole work is to carry
feeling, so it must be lit *with* feeling. Any fire will light a wick; only a *keeper's* fire,
given with a moment of genuine attention — a real thought, a real feeling of the day, spent on
that lamp — makes it a carrying lamp. A lamp lit without meaning gutters by midnight: there is
nothing in it to carry. This is the lamplighter's private, nightly version of the telling:
instead of giving his heart to the Heartlight, he spends it in small coin into the lamps and the
lighter, every night of his life. Grandmother never explained "what meaning it involves" because
it can't be explained, only paid.

If a lamp goes dark overnight, nothing dramatic happens — which is exactly why the duty needs
ritual discipline. One dark night is a dull morning. A dark season is a street that slowly cools
toward itself — flatter greetings, doors shut a little earlier. The Hush is the instantaneous,
total version of an ill every villager already knows in miniature.

### Why one family
The seed-flame stays lit through **unbroken keeping**: it must always be *claimed* — carried by
someone who tends it and means to pass it on. Hand-to-hand succession inside one household is
how the claim never lapses. The office also carries a real cost that can't be casually shared
(see §3: lamplighters are forbidden the telling — they carry their own hearts whole), so it
concentrates in a family that raises its children to bear it. Lake's line has held Emberbrook's
lighter since the gate closed.

### The brass lighter
The lighter is not a tool for making fire; it is a **seed-ember of the Kindling itself** — the
same flame that lit the Heartlight, kept small and portable, the Order's original walking-fire.
It burns no fuel. It never goes out because "out" is not something that happens to carried
light: it stays lit as long as it is *kept* — claimed, tended, meant. It would die only if its
keeper deliberately quenched it, or if it were abandoned with no one taking it up. Grandmother's
"it wasn't hers to put out" is Order law stated as kitchen wisdom: a keeper carries the flame;
the flame belongs to the Kindling. (This is the gun on the mantel for Chapter Ten.)

The lighter is a **dual vessel**, and the second half of that is a secret even from Lake. As a
seed of the Kindling on the Heartlight's line, it is a *walking piece of the communal warmth* —
the one hearth in the valley that travels: wherever the lamplighter carries it, the village's
radiance goes with him. And because the nightly "meaning it" spends small pieces of the
lamplighter's *own* feeling into the brass, night after night for a lifetime, it is also the one
vault of **Lake's own heart**. He stands outside the communal telling — and has been unknowingly
banking himself into the lighter his whole life.

Stated plainly, because the arc leans on it: **ordinary villagers cannot wield this fire.** An
unmeant flame gutters by midnight; carrying takes the consecrated nightly practice, paid for
years. And a villager who strays beyond the lamplight dims — their feeling lives in the flame,
and only a practiced keeper can carry a seed of it out to them. The lamplighter is not a man
with a torch; he is the only person in the valley the warmth will travel with.

Lake took the lighter up the night his grandmother died and has carried it every dusk since. He never swore
the Keeper's vow — but *carrying is claiming*, and that unbroken year of rounds is why the
lighter, alone in the valley, was not takeable on Emberwake.

### Emberwake and the Kindling Hour
Emberwake, the last night of autumn, is the anniversary of the founding fire — the "wake of the
ember" in both senses: the trail of the carried ember that founded the village, and a vigil for
the year now ending. In the old days it was the night the circuit-walkers arrived from the
Kindling to renew the Heartlight. The walkers stopped coming three centuries ago; the festival
stayed. The **Kindling Hour** is the one hour of the year the Heartlight *opens* — reaches back
along its old line toward the mother-fire — and the whole village queues up to make the year's
tellings: "bring a memory worth keeping." You tell the flame your year; you keep the memory; the
flame takes what it weighed, and everyone is warmer for it. Nothing starts until the lamps are
lit, because you do not open the village's heart with the ring unwarded. Hence Poppy: Lake is
the only soul allowed to be late tonight.

## 3. Feeling and Flame — the metaphysics, stated plainly

**Ordinary memory is untouched by any of this.** Everyone in the valley keeps their own factual
memories, in their own heads, everywhere, always — same as a traveler, same as a flameless town.
What a keeping flame takes, holds, and gives back is something else: the **felt residue** of
experience. Joys. Griefs given over. The trust rebuilt after a quarrel. Love of a place and its
people, commitments, understandings, hard-won wisdom. Not a ledger of moments — a shared heart.

**The telling** is a gift in one direction and a radiance in the other. You tell the flame a
memory; you keep the memory; the flame takes what it *meant* — and shines that meaning back out
on everyone in the light, forever. One villager's banked joy becomes the whole street's warmth;
one grief, given over, stops being a private stone and becomes something the whole village
quietly carries together. This is why the tradition exists and why it is worth a family's whole
line to tend it: villages with a Heartlight are *knit*. They hold together, they do not repeat
their old mistakes (the understanding that ended a feud stays alive in the light long after the
feud's details bore everyone), and they get up in the morning knowing why.

**Proximity is the whole game.** The radiance reaches you through fire on the Heartlight's line
— the lamps. Inside the ring of light: anchored. Outside it: living on your own heart. Travelers
and flameless towns are perfectly fine — a little lonelier, running on what they carry, the way
everyone did before the Kindling was found. But a *villager* who has spent a lifetime giving
feeling over has banked most of their heart, and dims when they stray beyond the lamplight —
which is why nobody wanders far, and why only a practiced keeper, carrying a meant seed, can
bring the warmth out to them (§2, the lighter). "Dark lamp, dull street" is literal.

**Anonymity is built in.** A feeling dissolves into the flame like water into water. Nothing can
be browsed, revisited, or traced to its teller. The flame has no index, keeps no moments, and
answers no questions. It only warms.

**Names.** A child's name is the **first telling**: every Emberbrook baby is named aloud to the
Heartlight. What the flame takes is not the word — it is the *bond*: the belonging of that name
to that child, and of that child to the village. A name told to the flame sits in the communal
warmth for life; villagers feel *addressed* by the light. This is why the Hush's signature
injury is a villager holding their own name like a coin found in the road: they can say the
word; it doesn't feel like theirs.

**The Hush, precisely.** The Hush is not amnesia; it is *unmooring*. When the flame left, every
fact stayed and every mooring went. Villagers keep their skills, their histories, the words of
their own names — and lose the feelings, motivations, commitments, and connections those facts
were anchored to. They are not blank; they are *unattached*: lost, unmotivated, disconnected
from the people around them, reading their own lives like paperwork.

**Who stood outside the Hush on Emberwake, and why:**
- **Vesper** is untouched because none of her feeling was ever in this flame. She arrived an
  hour ago and told Emberbrook's flame nothing; her heart is her own, same as any traveler's.
  (Her deeper connection to the *Kindling* is separate — §4.)
- **Lake** is untouched, for two stacked reasons. First, Order law: **the keeper must stand
  outside the kept** — a lamplighter is forbidden the telling and carries his own heart whole,
  because someone must still *feel the duty* on the day the flame fails. Nothing of Lake was
  ever in the Heartlight. Second, his trade's small nightly spendings live in the **lighter**,
  which is claimed and kept and therefore untouchable — "My lighter's still warm... Not this
  one." Both facts are true; Lake only knows the second one yet.
- **Rowan** half-resists: forty years of the ledger live in his hands and habits — a commitment
  worn so deep into the body that "I keep the ledger" still feels true when nothing else does.
  But his name was told at his naming, and the bond of it went with the rest: he knows the word
  and cannot make it his.
- **The villagers** keep their bodies' knowledge and their heads' facts, and lose *why any of it
  mattered, and to whom*. They still walk, bake, tie knots — they've lost being someone doing
  it, with someone, for someone.
- **Mochi** keeps everything, improved. Cats never tell anything to anyone; a cat's whole self —
  memory and heart alike — lives in the body, "where moths can't reach." Every lamplighter
  household keeps a cat for exactly this: cats see moths, hunt moths, and keep them off
  sleepers. Grandmother's saying is professional literature.

**Rowan's ledger** is the counterexample that proves the model: ink keeps *facts*, and facts
survive. Every page is exactly as legible the morning after the Hush as the morning before —
that is the tragedy of it. A book full of true things nobody feels: weddings that read like
weather reports, a flood that reads like an invoice. Ink is a rack to hang feeling on, and the
feelings have left the building. (Vesper's post-Hush notebook matters for the mirrored reason —
§4.)

**Moths** are stray, unanchored feeling. When a keeping flame dies or is broken open, its warmth
does not vanish — it scatters, and each stray feeling takes wing as a pale moth. A moth is
homeless and hungry: it seeks warmth to stay aloft, nestling into unwarded light and sleeping
minds and fraying what it touches — so the folk belief that "moths eat light" and the truth that
"moths *are* light" are the same fact. On the road beyond the gate: "That's where the light
went. Some of it never made it to the sky." Stray moths can be gathered home by a keeper's meant
flame — this becomes a core mechanic and a Chapter Seven turn.

**Animals feel keeping-fire the way they feel weather.** The fish circling the pond all day were
feeling the Heartlight's line strain as the recall gathered; they stopped the moment it snapped.
Mochi has been watching the north road all evening for the same reason.

## 4. The Hush, the Warden, and the Whisperwood — what actually happened

**The Warden of the Kindling** has tended the failing mother-fire alone for three hundred years.
He fed it first with himself — nearly all of his own heart is poured in, every joy and grief he
ever kept, which is why he is hooded, silent, and unhurried, and why his lantern-light burns
**pale blue**: keeping-fire with no one left to warm it burns cold. When he ran out of self, he
began **the recalls**: by the Order's oldest law, every Heartlight is a *loan* from the
Kindling, and a light whose keeper's line has failed is a lamp left burning in an empty house —
moths will scatter it; better the Warden carry the valley's heart home and pour it into the
mother-fire, where it will at least keep *something* burning. He has emptied other valleys this
way. He does not consider it theft. He grieves at every one. He bows.

**Why Emberbrook, why now:** Grandmother died a year ago, and Lake never swore the vow. For one
year, by the letter of the law the Warden serves, Emberbrook's light stood *unclaimed*. (That
Lake's nightly rounds were a claiming-in-deed is an argument the Warden will lose later — but
he wasn't checking deeds; he was reading the register of vows.) **Why on Emberwake:** the
Kindling Hour is the one hour the Heartlight opens its old line to the mother-fire. The Warden
did not break in; he stood at the far end of an open door and *recalled the loan*. The whole
heart of the village — the warmth in its lamps, the Heartlight, every bond and every banked
year — went down the line into his lantern between one heartbeat and the next. "His lantern
isn't dark — it's FULL." He bowed to the lighter because the lighter is validly claimed: a debt
collector's courtesy toward the one title in the valley he cannot touch. This is also why
Vesper's guilt-free and Lake's arc has a knife in it: Lake will eventually learn that his year
of doubt was the legal opening — and must be talked out of carrying that as fault. The law was
wrong, not the boy.

**Why carrying the Last Spark to the Kindling can fix it:** light is only ever carried — so
Emberbrook's heart is not destroyed; it is *in transit*, held in shape inside the Warden's
lantern, on foot (a full lantern moves at the pace of its carrier; keeping-fire cannot be
rushed). **Once poured into the Kindling, it dissolves into the general fire and can never be
re-separated** — that is the arc's hard deadline, and why the quest is a race, not a chase:
reach the Kindling before the pour. The second clock is slower and crueler: an unanchored
village *frays*. Letters from home arrive flatter, not blanker — all news, no warmth;
commitments quietly dissolving; Mara caring for Pip out of a duty that is wearing thin.
Restoration needs three things: a **sibling ember** (a seed lit from the same mother, to be the
new hearth the warmth can come home to), a **keeper** to hold it meant, and **people who can
re-anchor the village** — who can stand at the rekindled flame and say who everyone is *to each
other*, so the bonds take hold again. Names called at the new flame are bonds renewed, not
records retrieved. This is why Vesper's post-Hush notebook ("a copy of everyone" — who people
are to each other, the seeds of re-anchoring) and Rowan's ledger of facts become, literally,
the key items of the endgame.

**Vesper's dreams:** Vesper was born in **Ashfield**, a valley the Warden recalled when she was six.
Her name had been told to Ashfield's Heartlight at her naming; when that light was poured into
the Kindling, the belonging of six-year-old Vesper — her childhood's whole feeling of home —
went into the mother-fire, and it has been dreaming back at her ever since: forty-one drawings
of the clearing, the road "she had only ever seen with her eyes closed," the waystone, the gate
in the corner of drawing forty-one. The Hush could not touch her because each flame holds only
what it was given — Emberbrook's flame never held a feather's weight of her. But the Kindling
holds a six-year-old's belonging, and it has been sending her mail for twenty years. Her family
became wanderers after Ashfield went quiet and never spoke of it; what a six-year-old's memory
kept of one grey year has long since faded the ordinary way, and the reveal lands mid-arc. Her
unexplained certainty in the pact scene — *"It won't."* — is the piece of her in the fire,
answering.

## 5. The Arc — Chapters 2–10

The party travels the Whisperwood along the Order's dead circuit, gaining companions FF-style,
racing a walker who cannot be outrun, only intercepted — while letters from home arrive flatter
and flatter: all news, no warmth.

- **Ch. 2 — The Lanternstead.** First road chapter; full script in `docs/chapter2-script.md`. The
  pair learn the road lamp to lamp — Lake relights the Order's dead road-lamps, moths pooling in
  the dark stretches — and the Warden first appears: full pale-blue lantern far up the road, a
  bow to Lake's lighter, gone between blinks; Mochi's first hiss. At dusk they reach the Order's
  waystation and **Tally** — round, bespectacled, endlessly cheerful last friar, keeper of every
  rite and a great-lantern he has never seen burn ("You're real. The office is real. I have the
  whole liturgy and nobody ever came."). The night's first moth swarm comes for the lighter; the
  pair relight the great-lantern together, wick and winch; Tally weeps laughing at his first
  living light, and the lighter travels hooded after dark. Rowan's first letter arrives by grey
  post-crow — perfectly informative, strangely flat. Tally joins; the road points to Harrowdel.
- **Ch. 3 — Harrowdel.** A living valley whose elderly keeper is failing — slow decline shown in
  miniature, the stakes made ordinary. The Warden arrives and recalls Harrowdel before their
  eyes; first face-to-face; first bow. **Sable**, Harrowdel's heretic moth-catcher — who traps
  strays and returns stray warmth to people jar by jar — joins in cold fury.
- **Ch. 4 — The Ferry.** **Marrow** the ferryman takes payment only in memories and has grown
  rich and numb. The party pays; Marrow *feels* the memory — first thing in years — and poles
  after them. Hook: "You've paid me before. Long ago. Small girl, going the other way."
- **Ch. 5 — Ashfield.** The grey valley is Vesper's birthplace. Midpoint reveal: her dreams are
  her own childhood belonging, calling from the mother-fire. Mochi finds the house. The lighter
  flares at the cold pedestal. Vesper's chapter; Lake holds the lantern-watch and finally says
  grandmother's sayings out loud as *teaching*, not grief.
- **Ch. 6 — The Parley.** The Warden speaks. The Kindling is dying; every Heartlight in every
  living valley dies with it; he offers Lake the succession — Warden after him — and asks for
  the lighter as the last free seed. The creed schism made explicit: same creed, opposite
  obligations. Refusal costs them: home letters arrive drained — news without one feeling in
  it. Lake learns about the unclaimed year and takes the knife of it.
- **Ch. 7 — The Mothway.** Lake's chapter, opening on the Parley's knife still in him. The
  lighter is lost — or guttering; the chapter keeps it ambiguous whether Lake, wounded by the
  unclaimed year, half-let it happen ("maybe it was never mine"). For the first time the grey of
  the road reaches *him*: unmoored-Lake, facts intact, caring gone. **Vesper re-anchors him** —
  the "copy of everyone" notebook gets its first use on her own partner, the record-keeper of
  true things telling a keeper who he is, page by page, until it lands *felt* again — and helps
  him recover the lighter and the mission. Resolution: he relights it **from within**, finally
  understanding "mean it" — the flame was never the brass, the meaning was always *spending
  something true* — and that same understanding *is* the discovery of the counter-move: amid the
  storm-migration of moths (revealed: not vermin — stray unanchored *feeling*), Sable's craft
  plus Lake's newly meant fire learns that a meant flame calls moths home the way lamps ward
  them off. Stray warmth of Emberbrook is recovered and sent back. One crisis, three payoffs.
  *(Design note: deliberate mirror — Ch. 5 Ashfield is Vesper's identity crisis with Lake
  anchoring her; Ch. 7 is Lake's with Vesper anchoring him. Each saves the other exactly once —
  couch co-op thematics.)*
- **Ch. 8 — The Gatehouse.** The Warden's home: the archive of poured valleys, the ledger of
  recalls, the almost-empty man himself glimpsed off-duty. The mechanism of restoration is
  confirmed — a sibling ember, a keeper to hold it, and people to re-anchor the village name by
  name — turning Vesper's notebook and Rowan's ledger into the endgame keys. Marrow repays his
  debt of feeling.
- **Ch. 9 — The Pouring.** The clearing of drawing forty-one. The Kindling is nearly ash. The
  Warden begins the pour; the party arrives mid-stream; and the true dilemma lands — stopping
  him saves Emberbrook and starves the mother-fire, dooming every other valley. Nobody is
  wrong. Cliffhanger on the choice.
- **Ch. 10 — The Long Rekindling.** The answer was on the mantel all along: *it was never hers —
  or his — to put out, because it was always meant to go home.* Lake can return the lighter to
  the stone bowl because after Chapter Seven he is a keeper who no longer needs the vessel — the
  flame was never the brass. The seed — three centuries of nightly meant-fire, freely kept —
  rekindles the mother-flame. Emberbrook is called home out of the lantern name by name — every
  name a bond renewed at the new flame, not a record retrieved — Vesper and Rowan's ink the seeds of
  re-anchoring (Pip first). The Warden pours the last of himself to bridge the rekindling, and
  the party tells the new fire his story — so the weight of him stays warm in the light. The fix
  is structural, not magical: the circuit is walked again. Vesper — Waykeeper — charts it; her
  piece comes home; she finally dreams her own dreams. Coda: next Emberwake, two keepers at the
  Kindling Hour, Mochi on the pedestal.

## 6. Characters

**Vesper** — traveling mapmaker, late twenties, eleven days' walk from anywhere on her charts.
Professional creed: *keep records of true things.* Quick-tongued, precise, allergic to
mysticism — which is a problem, because since age six she has drawn the same clearing in her
sleep, forty-one times, and refuses to be strange about it. She came to Emberbrook to make the
dreams answer for themselves ("Something has been sending me MAIL. I intend to answer it in
person"). What she doesn't know: she is Ashfield's last named child, and the fire that holds her
childhood belonging has been calling her home for twenty years to be its Waykeeper. Her arc runs
from charting places to charting *people* — the post-Hush notebook, "a copy of everyone," who
they are and who they are to each other, begun in stubborn pity, is used first on the villagers,
then (Ch. 7, its proving) on her own unmoored partner, and finally as the literal instrument of
the ending. Signature habits: numbers her drawings, counts her honeybuns dishonestly, writes
things down mid-catastrophe.

**Lake** — the last lamplighter of Emberbrook, mid-twenties, quiet, apologizes to lamps. Raised
by his grandmother in the Order's household discipline: the rounds, the sayings, the cat, and
the one hard rule he only half-understood — *we don't tell the flame; we keep our own.* Which
means his grief is different from everyone else's: they give their griefs to the flame and are
given warmth back; his grandmother's loss lives only in him, whole and unshared, in a handful of
sayings he can't stop reciting. A year ago she set the lighter down; he picked it up and did the
rounds every dusk since — but never swore the vow, because he wasn't sure he believed, and it
felt like lying to a fire. What being lamplighter means to him at curtain-rise: a costume he's
wearing for a dead woman. The doubt comes due twice: his unsworn year turns out to be the
Warden's legal opening (the Ch. 6 knife), and in Chapter Seven it nearly takes the lighter
itself — his crisis, mirrored on Vesper's at Ashfield, and it is Vesper who anchors him back
(that is the story's job, and mostly hers: make him put the knife down). What the office means
by Chapter Ten: the thing he was quietly doing all along — carrying, no vessel required. He
knows every window in the village and every name behind it, because remembering people the hard
way is the family trade.

**Rowan** — village elder and ledger-keeper; runs Emberbrook by cheerfully denying he runs it.
Forty years of ledger live in his hands, which is why "I keep the ledger" is the one thing that
still feels true after the Hush — while his own name sits in his mouth like a borrowed word.
Practical in catastrophe, funny on purpose, and the closest thing to an Order scholar the valley
has left — he knows the *words* of the old rites without their reasons.
**Poppy** — baker; hospitality-as-law ("guests eat first — that is LAW on Emberwake"); burns her
thumb on the first tray every morning and swears she won't tomorrow. Post-Hush she rebuilds
herself from a row of words: *honeybuns, Poppy, thumb.*
**Mara** — Pip's mother; steady, unsentimental warmth; waited out a snowstorm at the pass for
him to be born. Post-Hush she believes every fact about her son and feels none of them — the
Hush's cruelest exhibit, and the engine of the letters-from-home thread.
**Pip** — seven; orbits people he likes; owns one good stick. The first villager to put the
counter-move into practice: "I'm teaching her me again." Believes in ink because Vesper told him
to; the rekindled flame gives him his mother back — her love of him renewed by name — in
Chapter Ten, first.
**Finn** — fisherman; prefers fish to festivals; hands know the knots. The valley's accidental
instrument-reader: his circling fish are the only warning anyone got.
**Mochi** — grandmother's cat, technically; left the cottage the year she died and hired himself
to Vesper at the waystone (a cat recognizing a Waykeeper is old Order staffing procedure).
Moth-warden: sees them, hunts them, keeps them off sleepers. Keeps his entire self in the body,
where moths can't reach. Acknowledges Lake as staff.
**The Warden (the Stranger)** — last officer of the Order, keeper of the Kindling, three hundred
years at his post, nearly all of himself poured into the fire he serves. Pale-blue lantern:
keeping-fire with no one left to warm it. Courteous the way a debt collector is courteous; bows
to valid claims; grieves every recall and performs the next one. Not evil — *loyal*, to the
letter of a law written for a world that still walked its circuits. He is what a lamplighter
becomes who keeps the flame and lets go of everything the flame was for. He is also, until
Chapter Ten, the only person alive who knows the whole truth.

## 7. What the Player Should Understand When

**Plain knowledge — villagers say this casually, like weather.** Lamps keep the streets
warm-hearted; a dark lamp means a dull, grey-feeling morning. At Emberwake you bring a memory
worth keeping and tell it to the flame; you keep the memory, the flame keeps the heart of it
forever and shines it back on everybody, as long as you live in the light. The lamplighter's
family tends the flame, does the rounds at dusk, and — everyone knows this, nobody thinks it's
odd — never makes tellings themselves. Moths are bad news; cats are good news; nothing starts
until the lamps are lit. Kids' folklore is accurate: Renn is *right* that you can see the
tellings go in. **Dialogue rule: none of this is mysterious to anyone on-screen. Write it warm,
proud, and matter-of-fact.**

**Tradition — known but not understood.** Why the keeper stands outside the kept; why the
lighter never goes out; "it wasn't hers to put out"; "light is only ever carried"; the Old
Gate's rules, the twin sigils, and "that road was meant to be walked by two"; "our flame was
first drawn from a shrine deep in the Whisperwood." Villagers repeat these like proverbs —
grandmother's sayings, Rowan's rites — with total confidence and no idea of the machinery.
**Dialogue rule: characters state these plainly and are cheerfully unable to explain them.**

**Mystery — reserved, with reveal chapters.** Moths are stray feeling (7, seeded from Ch. 1).
The recall law and the unclaimed year (6). The Warden's identity, history, and the dying
Kindling (6–8). Ashfield, and Vesper's dreams as her own belonging calling (4–5, seeded by
Marrow). Re-anchoring — names as bonds renewed at a sibling flame — as the mechanism of
restoration (8). The lighter as the Kindling's own seed and the meaning of returning it (10).
**Dialogue rule: nobody hints at these early — the early chapters play fair by being genuinely
warm and plain, so the Hush lands on a real village, not a spooky one.**

### Canon quick-reference (for writers)
1. Light does not die; it is only ever carried. Every plot beat obeys this — including the Hush,
   which is a *transfer*, and the ending, which is a *return*.
2. A flame keeps only what it is told — and what it keeps is the *feeling*, never the fact.
   Memory stays with its owner, always and everywhere; the flame pools the given-over heart of
   things and rains it back through the lamps. Ink keeps facts; bodies keep skills; only flame
   keeps meaning — and what the flame keeps, the flame can lose, or have carried away.
3. The Hush is unmooring, never amnesia. Post-Hush villagers know every fact and feel none of
   them. Write dissociation ("it doesn't feel like mine"), never failed recall ("I can't
   remember the fact").
4. Keepers stand outside the kept: lamplighters never tell the flame, so they alone carry their
   own hearts whole — and grieve the old way.
5. Claiming is keeping: the lighter stays lit, and untouchable, because Lake carried it — vow or
   no vow. The law reads registers; the fire reads deeds. The fire is right.
6. The Warden is owed sympathy at every appearance. He bows. He is never sneered at by the
   script, only argued with.
